5  From Sample to Population - Understanding Randomness, Sampling, and Inference

5.1 Sampling Methods

Probability Sampling

Probability sampling methods involve random selection, giving each member of the population a known, non-zero chance of being selected. These methods allow researchers to calculate sampling error and make statistical inferences about the population.

  1. Simple Random Sampling (SRS)
    • Definition: Each member of the population has an equal chance of being selected.
    • Advantages: Minimizes selection bias; allows straightforward statistical analysis.
    • Disadvantages: Requires a complete sampling frame; may not capture enough members of smaller subgroups.
    • Example: To select 100 students from a university with 10,000 students, assign each student a number and use a random number generator to select 100 numbers.
    • Best used when: The population is relatively homogeneous and a complete list of population members is available.
  2. Stratified Random Sampling
    • Definition: The population is divided into mutually exclusive subgroups (strata) based on shared characteristics, then samples are randomly selected from each stratum.
    • Advantages: Ensures representation of key subgroups; can improve precision for same sample size as SRS; allows analysis within and between strata.
    • Disadvantages: Requires prior knowledge of population characteristics for stratification; more complex analysis.
    • Example: In a national political survey, divide the population into strata based on geographic regions (Northeast, Midwest, South, West) and then randomly sample from each region proportionally to their population size.
    • Best used when: The population contains distinct subgroups that might respond differently to the research question.
  3. Cluster Sampling
    • Definition: The population is divided into clusters (usually geographic), some clusters are randomly selected, and all members within those clusters are studied.
    • Advantages: More cost-effective when population is geographically dispersed; doesn’t require a complete list of population members.
    • Disadvantages: Lower statistical precision than SRS or stratified sampling; clusters must be representative.
    • Example: To study high school students’ study habits, randomly select 20 high schools from across the country and survey all students in those schools.
    • Best used when: The population is widely dispersed geographically and traveling to all units would be costly.
  4. Systematic Sampling
    • Definition: Selecting every kth item from a list after a random start.
    • Advantages: Simple to implement; often more practical than SRS; can avoid neighbor effects.
    • Disadvantages: Can introduce bias if there’s a periodic pattern in the list.
    • Example: At a busy shopping mall, survey every 20th person who enters, starting with a randomly chosen number between 1 and 20.
    • Best used when: The population is ordered randomly or in a way unrelated to the study variables.
  5. Multi-stage Sampling
    • Definition: Combining multiple sampling methods in stages.
    • Advantages: Practical for large-scale surveys; balances cost and precision.
    • Disadvantages: Complex design and analysis; multiple stages can compound sampling errors.
    • Example: First randomly select counties (cluster sampling), then randomly select households within those counties (simple random sampling), and finally select one adult from each household (systematic sampling).
    • Best used when: Studying large, complex populations across wide geographical areas.

Non-probability Sampling

Non-probability sampling doesn’t involve random selection, which means statistical inferences about the population must be made with caution. While it can introduce bias, it may be necessary in certain situations.

  1. Convenience Sampling
    • Definition: Selecting easily accessible subjects.
    • Advantages: Fast, inexpensive, and easy to implement.
    • Disadvantages: High risk of selection bias; limits generalizability.
    • Example: A researcher studying college students’ sleep patterns might survey students in their own classes.
    • Best used for: Pilot studies, exploratory research, or when resources are severely limited.
  2. Purposive Sampling
    • Definition: Selecting subjects based on specific characteristics relevant to the research question.
    • Advantages: Focuses on relevant cases; useful for in-depth studies of specific groups.
    • Disadvantages: Researcher bias in selection; limited generalizability.
    • Example: For a study on the experiences of CEOs in the tech industry, intentionally seek out and interview CEOs from various tech companies.
    • Best used for: Qualitative research, case studies, or studying unique populations.
  3. Snowball Sampling
    • Definition: Participants recruit other participants from their networks.
    • Advantages: Access to hard-to-reach or hidden populations; builds on social networks.
    • Disadvantages: Sample biased toward those in certain social networks; cannot calculate selection probabilities.
    • Example: In a study of undocumented immigrants’ access to healthcare, researchers ask initial participants to refer other potential participants.
    • Best used for: Studying rare populations or sensitive topics where no sampling frame exists.
  4. Quota Sampling
    • Definition: Selecting participants to meet specific quotas for certain characteristics to match known population parameters.
    • Advantages: Ensures representation of key demographic groups; faster and cheaper than probability sampling; doesn’t require sampling frame.
    • Disadvantages: Non-random selection within quotas can introduce bias; inference is limited.
    • Example: In a market research study, researchers ensure they interview specific numbers of people from different age groups, genders, and income levels.
    • Best used for: Commercial polling, market research, or when probability sampling is not feasible.

Why Pollsters Increasingly Use Quota Sampling

In recent years, many polling organizations have shifted toward quota sampling approaches for several key reasons:

  1. Declining Response Rates: Traditional probability-based telephone polls have seen response rates drop from about 36% in the 1990s to less than 10% today. This increases costs and potentially introduces non-response bias that can be worse than selection bias from non-probability methods.

  2. Coverage Issues: Random digit dialing no longer reaches a representative sample of the population as many people have abandoned landlines for cell phones, and many don’t answer calls from unknown numbers.

  3. Cost Efficiency: Probability-based polls have become prohibitively expensive as response rates decline, while online panels and quota sampling are more affordable.

  4. Speed: In fast-moving political campaigns or rapidly evolving public opinion situations, quota sampling can deliver results much faster than probability methods.

  5. Weighting and Modeling Improvements: Modern statistical techniques allow pollsters to adjust quota samples to better represent the target population by weighting responses based on known population parameters.

  6. Hybrid Approaches: Many pollsters now use hybrid methods that combine elements of probability and non-probability sampling, with sophisticated weighting and modeling to improve accuracy.

The 2016 US presidential election, where many polls failed to predict the outcome accurately, led to considerable soul-searching among pollsters. Rather than abandoning quota sampling, many organizations have refined their methods, focusing on better quota definitions, improved weighting techniques, and more transparent reporting of methodological limitations.

Despite these trends, it’s important to note that probability sampling remains the gold standard for statistical inference. Well-designed probability samples still provide the most reliable foundation for generalizing from sample to population, especially for academic research where accuracy is prioritized over cost and speed.

5.2 Survey Errors: Understanding What Can Go Wrong in Research

Why Error Typologies Matter

In social science research, errors are inevitable. However, different types of errors require different solutions. Understanding these typologies helps researchers:

  • Identify the source of potential problems
  • Design better studies to minimize errors
  • Interpret results more accurately
  • Communicate limitations honestly

5.3 First Typology: Systematic vs. Random Error

Systematic Error (Bias)

Definition: Consistent, predictable errors that push results in one direction away from the true value.

Key characteristics: - Always occurs in the same direction - Cannot be reduced by increasing sample size - Affects accuracy (how close we are to the true value) - Often caused by flawed research design or implementation

Examples: - Leading questions: “Don’t you think the government should reduce wasteful spending?” (pushes toward “yes” responses) - Social desirability bias: People underreport socially unacceptable behaviors (drug use, tax evasion) - Coverage bias: Phone surveys missing people without landlines systematically excludes younger populations - Interviewer bias: Interviewers unconsciously influencing responses based on their expectations

Warning

Critical point: You cannot fix systematic error by collecting more data. If your thermometer always reads 2 degrees too high, taking 1,000 measurements won’t give you the correct temperature – it will give you a very precise wrong answer.

Random Error

Definition: Unpredictable errors that vary randomly around the true value, sometimes above, sometimes below.

Key characteristics: - No consistent direction - Decreases as sample size increases (law of large numbers) - Affects precision (consistency of measurements) - Often caused by chance factors or measurement limitations

Examples: - Sampling variability: Different random samples giving slightly different results - Question wording ambiguity: Some respondents interpreting a question one way, others differently - Interviewer differences: Natural variation between different interviewers - Respondent mood: People’s responses varying based on their mood when surveyed

Tip

Good news: Random errors tend to cancel out with larger samples. If some measurements are too high and others too low by random chance, the average gets closer to the true value as you add more observations.

Visual Analogy: Target Practice

Think of research as shooting arrows at a target, where the bullseye represents the true value:

Error Type Pattern Description
Low systematic, Low random All arrows clustered near bullseye Ideal: Accurate and precise
Low systematic, High random Arrows scattered around bullseye Precise on average: Unbiased but inconsistent
High systematic, Low random Arrows clustered away from bullseye Consistently wrong: Biased but precise
High systematic, High random Arrows scattered far from bullseye Worst case: Both biased and imprecise

5.4 Second Typology: Sampling vs. Non-Sampling Errors

Sampling Errors

Definition: Errors that occur because we study a sample instead of the entire population.

Key characteristics: - Inevitable when using samples (unless studying entire population) - Can be estimated and quantified - Decrease with larger sample sizes - Addressed by confidence intervals and margins of error

Types: - Random sampling error: Natural variation between samples - Sampling frame error: When your list of potential respondents doesn’t match the target population - Selection bias: When certain groups are systematically more or less likely to be included

Examples: - A political poll showing 52% support, but the true population value is 50% - Missing 18-25 year-olds because they’re not in your phone directory - Higher response rates among retirees than working adults

Note

Remember: Even perfect sampling will produce some error because a sample is not the entire population. This is why we report margins of error (e.g., “±3 percentage points”).

Non-Sampling Errors

Definition: All other errors that can occur in research, regardless of whether you’re using a sample or studying the entire population.

Key characteristics: - Can occur even with a census (complete enumeration) - Often more serious than sampling errors - Difficult to quantify - Not reduced by increasing sample size

Major categories:

Coverage Errors: - Under-coverage: Missing parts of the target population (people without phones, homeless individuals) - Over-coverage: Including units outside the target population (non-citizens in a citizen survey)

Measurement Errors: - Question problems: Ambiguous, leading, or sensitive questions - Response errors: Misunderstanding, memory problems, social desirability - Interviewer effects: How the interviewer influences responses - Mode effects: Differences between phone, online, and face-to-face surveys

Nonresponse Errors: - Unit nonresponse: Entire individuals/households not participating - Item nonresponse: Participants skipping specific questions - Response rate bias: When nonrespondents differ systematically from respondents

Processing Errors: - Data entry mistakes: Incorrectly coding responses - Editing errors: “Correcting” data incorrectly - Weighting errors: Improper adjustments for demographic differences

Important

Reality check: In most surveys, non-sampling errors are larger than sampling errors. A survey with a 3% margin of error might have 5-10% error from other sources.

5.5 The Total Survey Error Framework

Conceptual Foundation

The Total Survey Error framework recognizes that survey quality depends on minimizing all sources of error, not just sampling error. It provides a systematic way to think about every step of the research process.

Core principle: Total error = Sampling error + All non-sampling errors

The Framework in Practice

Survey Stage Potential Errors Solutions
Design Phase Coverage problems, question wording issues Pre-testing, expert review, pilot studies
Data Collection Nonresponse, interviewer bias, measurement error Multiple contact attempts, interviewer training, mixed-mode approaches
Data Processing Coding errors, editing mistakes Double data entry, consistency checks, automated validation
Analysis Weighting errors, inappropriate statistical methods Proper weight calculations, sensitivity analyses

Key Insights from TSE Framework

1. Trade-offs are everywhere: - Longer surveys get more information but higher nonresponse - Personal interviews reduce measurement error but increase costs - Online surveys are cheaper but may miss certain populations

2. Error sources interact: - Attempts to reduce one error might increase another - For example, callbacks to reduce nonresponse might increase social desirability bias (persistent respondents might be more compliant)

3. Focus on the biggest problems: - Don’t obsess over sampling error if measurement error is larger - A census with bad questions is worse than a good sample survey

4. Context matters: - Error tolerance depends on survey purpose - Political polls need different precision than academic research

Practical Guidelines for Researchers

1. During Survey Design: - Map all potential error sources for your specific study - Consider how each design choice affects different types of errors - Pre-test everything (questions, procedures, technology)

2. During Data Collection: - Monitor response rates and respondent characteristics in real-time - Document everything (who responded when, under what conditions) - Train staff to recognize and minimize their influence on responses

3. During Analysis: - Compare your sample to known population characteristics - Conduct sensitivity analyses: how do results change with different assumptions? - Be transparent about limitations

4. When Reporting: - Report more than just margin of error - Discuss potential biases and their likely direction - Acknowledge what you don’t know

Summary

Understanding error typologies helps researchers make better decisions at every stage. Remember:

  • Systematic errors require design changes, not bigger samples
  • Random errors can be reduced with larger samples
  • Sampling errors are often smaller than non-sampling errors
  • The Total Survey Error framework reminds us to consider all error sources, not just the ones that are easy to calculate

Good research isn’t about eliminating all errors (impossible), but about understanding, minimizing, and honestly reporting the errors that remain.
